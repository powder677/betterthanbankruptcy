Deleting My Ego: Why I Removed 'CEO' From My Resume

Failed entrepreneurs often wear the "Founder" badge with pride. But to a hiring manager, that badge signals flight risk. Here is how I dumbed down my resume to get hired.

After my logistics company folded, I thought my experience would be highly valued in the job market. I had managed payroll, negotiated supplier contracts, and led a team of 15. I was a "Founder." I was a "CEO." Surely, corporate America would fight to hire someone with that level of grit.

I applied to 50 jobs. I listed my title as "Owner/Founder of [Business Name]". I wrote a bio about my "visionary leadership" and "entrepreneurial spirit."

The result? Zero interviews. Not even a screening call. I was sending resumes into a black hole.

The Pivot

I finally got honest feedback from a recruiter friend. She told me, "When I see 'Former CEO' applying for a mid-level project manager role, I don't see talent. I see a headache. I see someone who is going to argue with their boss, try to rewrite our strategy on day one, and quit the moment they get funding for their next idea."

She called me a "Flight Risk."

It stung, but she was right. I was trying to impress them, but I was actually scaring them. I realized I needed to delete my ego to pay my rent. I rewrote my resume completely.

The "Ego" Version (Failed)

Title: Founder & CEO

Goal: Seeking visionary leadership roles to drive strategic growth.

Result: 0 Interviews

The "Employee" Version (Hired)

Title: Operations Manager

Goal: Seeking to support team execution through process optimization.

Result: 3 Interviews in 1 Week

I didn't lie about what I did—I still managed the payroll and the team—but I framed it as a function, not an identity. I removed the word "Founder" entirely.

The Result

The change was immediate. Within seven days of sending out the "dumbed down" resume, I had three interviews lined up.

In the interviews, I didn't talk about my "vision." I talked about how good I was at following SOPs (Standard Operating Procedures) and supporting department heads. I made them feel safe. I made them feel like I was there to work, not to take over.

I landed a mid-level Operations role. The pay was half of what I paid myself in the good years, and reporting to a manager ten years younger than me hurt my pride. But when the first direct deposit hit, my landlord didn't care about my pride. He just cared that the check cleared.

The Verdict

You are not applying for "Former CEO." You are applying for "Employee."

When you are rebuilding, your resume is not a trophy case; it is a marketing document. The goal is to solve the hiring manager's problem, not to prove how important you used to be. Tailor your story to make them feel safe, compliant, and useful.
